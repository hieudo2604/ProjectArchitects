# Final Presentation

## Scenario

You are interviewing for a software engineering role at a leading tech company. As part of the interview process, you have been asked to give a **10-minute presentation** about a software development project you worked on. The interview panel includes senior engineers and a hiring manager.

This is your chance to show that you can build real software, communicate technical decisions clearly, and reflect on your own work honestly.

## Your Task

Create a **slide deck** (Google Slides, PowerPoint, or Keynote) that walks the interview panel through a software project you have worked on. This can be a class project, personal project, hackathon project, or internship/work project.

If you have completed the project and you feel confident presenting and you must be specific and concrete in your technical details.

## Required Slides

Your deck should include the following sections. You do not need exactly one slide per section - use as many as you need to communicate clearly.

### 1. Title Slide
- Project name
- Your name
- One-line description of the project

### 2. Problem Statement
- What problem does this project solve?
- Who is the user or stakeholder?
- Why does this problem matter? (especially in the context of the company you are "interviewing" at)

### 3. Technical Overview
- High-level architecture (include a diagram - even a simple hand-drawn one is fine)
- Tech stack: languages, frameworks, databases, APIs, tools
- Why you chose this stack over alternatives

### 4. Your Role and Contributions
- What specifically did **you** build?
- If it was a team project, be clear about your individual contributions vs. the team's
- Interviewers want to know what **you** can do, not what your team did (in a team, everyone should list their contribution)

### 5. A Technical Deep Dive
- Pick **one** interesting or challenging part of the project and go deeper
- This could be an algorithm, a design pattern, a data model, a performance optimization, a tricky integration, etc.
- Show code snippets, pseudocode, or diagrams - not just words
- Explain your reasoning and trade-offs

### 6. Challenges and How You Solved Them
- What went wrong or was harder than expected?
- How did you debug, research, or iterate to a solution?
- Top tech companies value engineers who can work through hard problems. Please show that you can work through hard problems.

### 7. Results and Impact
- Does it work? Show a demo, screenshots, and metrics
- What was the outcome? (users, performance numbers, feedback, grade, etc.)
- If you could measure success, how would you?

### 8. What You Would Do Differently
- With hindsight, what would you change about your approach?
- What would you add if you had more time?
- This shows self-awareness and growth mindset that interviewers pay attention to this

### 9. Q&A Slide
- A closing slide inviting questions
- Include links to a GitHub repo, live demo, or documentation

## Presentation Guidelines

- **Length:** 10 minutes, followed by 5 minutes of Q&A
- **Slide count:** Aim for 10-15 slides. Do not cram text onto slides. Please use visuals, diagrams, and bullet points
- **No walls of text.** If a slide has more than 6 bullet points or 3 sentences, you have too much on it
- **Diagrams matter.** At minimum, include an architecture diagram and one technical diagram (flowchart, data model, sequence diagram, etc.)
- **Know your audience.** You are presenting to engineers. Be precise. Use correct terminology. Do not hand-wave over technical details
- **Practice.** You should be able to present without reading your slides. The slides support your talk and they are not a script

## What You Will Be Evaluated On

| Criteria | Weight |
|---|---|
| Clarity of problem statement and motivation | 15% |
| Technical depth and accuracy | 25% |
| Quality of architecture/design decisions | 20% |
| Honest reflection on challenges and trade-offs | 15% |
| Slide design and visual communication | 10% |
| Delivery and ability to answer questions | 15% |

## Tips From Real Interviewers

- "Tell me what **you** did, not what the tutorial told you to do."
- "I want to see that you made decisions, not just followed instructions."
- "If you hit a wall and solved it, that is more impressive than if everything went smoothly."
- "Show me you can explain something complex to someone who was not in the room when you built it."
- "The best candidates are honest about what they do not know."
